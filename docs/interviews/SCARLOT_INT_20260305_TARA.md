**BIAS FLAGS:**

1. **Written questionnaire bias**: Self-administered written responses lack the probing depth of live interviews. Tara controlled what to share and what to omit. The interviewer could not follow up on ambiguities, redirect away from abstractions, or probe emotional reactions. Multiple "What was not said" gaps (Section 08) may be artifacts of the format rather than genuine absences.

2. **Newcomer sampling bias**: At 3 months, Tara has not yet encountered the full spectrum of problems. Her low-friction perception of P3 (no inbound filtering) may reflect insufficient exposure rather than a genuine structural difference. Her signals should be weighted accordingly — they represent the newcomer experience, not a challenge to the established priority stack.

3. **Jurisdictional confound**: Tara operates in France, not Switzerland. Multiple signals (community fragmentation, "aucun systeme," market distortion by hobbyists, legal precarity) may be France-specific artifacts of client-criminalization law. These signals should not be used to adjust the Switzerland-focused priority stack without explicit segmentation.

4. **GFE model confound**: The GFE model creates unique dynamics (emotional investment, longer vetting, shame-driven ghosting) that may not generalize to other practice models (e.g., transactional, agency-based). Tara's extreme P6 (no-shows & time-wasters) signal (50-75% drop-off) may be model-specific rather than universal.

5. **Secondhand data on market distortion**: The "hobbyist practitioners" signal comes entirely from what clients told Tara. Clients have incentives to understate market rates and reference cheaper alternatives. This signal should be treated as a client negotiation tactic until corroborated by TDS peers.

6. **Survivorship bias on screening**: Tara reports confidence in her instinct-based screening, but at 3 months with occasional practice, her sample size is small. She may not yet have encountered scenarios where her instinct failed. The absence of negative safety experiences is not evidence of effective screening.

7. **Self-selection into questionnaire**: Tara chose to participate, accessed through the co-founder's trusted network. She may be more reflective, more articulate, or more motivated than the average newcomer TDS. Her willingness to share does not represent newcomer TDS generally.

**RESEARCHER NOTES:**

- This is the most data-sparse interview in the cohort so far. The written format and newcomer profile combine to produce fewer actionable signals than live interviews with experienced practitioners. However, the signals that do emerge are high-quality: the 50-75% ghost rate and "aucun systeme" are among the strongest individual data points in the research.

In Switzerland, where sex work is legal and regulated, the community infrastructure may be stronger — or it may not. The P3 (no inbound filtering) counter-signal is explained by newcomer sampling bias. The new signals (GFE loop, shame ghosting, market distortion, community fragmentation) are noted for future integration but require corroboration.
