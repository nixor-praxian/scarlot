## The Event

Academic colloquium (journée d'étude) at Sciences Po Paris, LIEPP lab, Salle G009, 28 rue des Saints-Pères. Day 2 of a two-day sequence. Day 1 (April 13) was the political event, a day of conferences and roundtables at the French Senate hosted with support from Paris senator Anne Souyris. Day 2 at Sciences Po was the academic restitution.

The event marked the 10th anniversary of France's Loi Prostitution (Law n° 2016-444, adopted April 13, 2016) and was organized around three research projects converging on the same question: does the current French legislative framework actually serve the people it claims to protect?

## The Three Projects Presented

### 1. ANR DPTS2026 (Droit(s) et Politique(s) du Travail Sexuel)
Centerpiece of the event. ANR-funded (€99,426) participatory action-research project coordinated by Benoît Schmaltz (CERCRID, Université Jean Monnet Saint-Étienne), bringing together sex worker associations (Fédération Parapluie Rouge, 15 member associations), Tullia, Médecins du Monde, and researchers in law, sociology, history, linguistics, and political science. Key output: a community-drafted proposition de loi, built bottom-up from a national consultation of sex workers, then formalized with legal scholars and law students. The project adopts a "droit des politiques publiques" approach, positioning the university as a "conseil de la société."

### 2. LIEPP Project: "Évaluer la pénalisation du proxénétisme en France"
Led by Hélène Le Bail (CNRS/CERI, Sciences Po) with Marie Mercat-Bruns and Mathilde Geoffroy. Evaluates how French pimping laws are implemented and their impact on sex workers, using jurisprudence analysis crossed with interviews of law enforcement and judicial professionals. Key finding, published as **LIEPP Working Paper n°174 (January 2025)**: French criminal law defines pimping so broadly that it criminalizes virtually all relationships sex workers maintain, both personal and professional; it complicates housing access, makes co-working impossible despite security benefits, and discourages contact with police.

### 3. Sex Worker Archives & Mobilization
Association Tullia's work on collecting, analyzing, and preserving archives of sex worker organizing and legislative history.

## Policy Context

### The 2016 Law and 10-Year Track Record

France adopted the Nordic model in 2016: the law abolished the soliciting offense, shifted criminalization to buyers (fines up to €3,750), and created a "parcours de sortie" for those wanting to exit prostitution. Ten years later, the verdict is deeply contested.

**Abolitionist camp** (Coalition pour l'abolition, Mouvement du Nid) claims concrete results: 10,000 clients arrested, 2,000 people exited prostitution, 65 networks dismantled in 2024.

**Rights-based camp** (STRASS, Médecins du Monde, Fédération Parapluie Rouge) sees the law as catastrophic. Médecins du Monde calls it "totally counterproductive." Concrete effects: 95% of identified victims now work in enclosed settings vs 5% on the street, making them harder to reach and more vulnerable. Sex workers report reduced bargaining power, more violence, pressure to accept unprotected sex, and lower rates.

### The ECHR Ruling (July 2024)

In July 2024, the European Court of Human Rights ruled that France's client penalization does not violate Article 8 (right to private life), granting wide margin of appreciation given the lack of European consensus on prostitution policy. Critically, the Court acknowledged being "fully conscious of the undeniable difficulties and risks" faced by sex workers, but attributed those to pre-existing conditions, not the law itself. Legal defeat for the sex workers' movement, but the Court's reasoning left analytical space open.

**Strategic consequence:** the CEDH ruling closed the judicial route. Everything after July 2024 becomes a legislative and discursive fight, not a constitutional one. This is when DPTS2026's strategy (build a community-drafted PPL with academic backing) became the only viable path.

### The Souyris-Kerbrat PPL (Proposition de Loi), April 13, 2026

Timed to the anniversary, senator Anne Souyris (Écologistes) and deputy Andy Kerbrat (LFI) presented a proposition de loi aiming to decriminalize sex work. This is the political vehicle for the DPTS2026 project's community-drafted law. Key provisions: repeal of client penalization and more broadly repeal of pimping laws.

Unprecedented element: for the first time, a French proposition de loi on sex work was co-drafted with the people who do the work, based on consultation with 70 sex workers. Supported by Fédération Parapluie Rouge, Amnesty International, Acceptess-T, NousToutes. The proposal aims to distinguish clearly between voluntary sex work and exploitation, strengthen anti-trafficking measures, and provide actual labor rights.

**Note on coalition cracks:** Kerbrat participated in the Senate colloquium but will not deposit the same text at the Assemblée due to substantive differences.

## European Landscape

Belgium decriminalized sex work in 2022, the only European country to do so. The Netherlands, Germany, and Austria have forms of legalized sex work. France and Sweden criminalize buying but not selling. Several European countries are re-examining their sex work policies, with some moving toward decriminalization and others reinforcing social protection.

**Belgium as operational reference point:** UTSOPI and Belgian justice ministry materials describe the decriminalization as specifically targeting "third parties" (accountants, drivers, landlords, bankers, graphic designers). The May 2024 Belgian employment law added a specific employment contract regime for sex workers with refusal rights, emergency buttons, and pre-approved employers.

## The Three-Camp Political Structure

The French landscape is not bipolar. It is genuinely polarized in multiple directions.

### Government camp (abolitionist, reinforced)
On April 13, 2026, minister Aurore Bergé convened the interministerial follow-up committee and reaffirmed France's "ferme, claire et constante" abolitionist position. Core talking points:
- ~37,000 sex workers in France
- +43% rise in registered minor victims between 2021 and 2025
- 86% of minor-involved situations start via internet/social media (OnlyFans, MYM cited by name)
- €7.5M allocated in 2026
- AFIS revalued to €559/month
- Legislative vehicle: backing the PPL Mercier on online sexual exploitation

### Decriminalist camp (Souyris-Kerbrat PPL)
Carried by Écologistes in the Senate and LFI in the Assemblée, backed by Fédération Parapluie Rouge, Amnesty, Acceptess-T, NousToutes, Médecins du Monde. Internal friction: Kerbrat won't deposit the same text at the Assemblée.

### Regulationist camp (new entrant)
- December 2025: RN deputy Jean-Philippe Tanguy prepared a PPL allowing sex workers to operate as cooperatives in "maisons closes"
- 2024: LR deputy Philippe Juvin deposited a PPL "to secure the rights of those who prostitute themselves to guarantee a safer practice"

The abolitionist camp now fights both the decriminalist left and the regulationist right simultaneously. Terrain that does not exist in Belgium, Sweden, or Germany.

## Full Timeline 2013-2026

### 2013-2016: The original battle
Four-year parliamentary fight. 2013 PPL deposited by Socialist deputy Bruno Le Roux, picked up by Najat Vallaud-Belkacem and Laurence Rossignol. The Senate rejected client penalization twice; the Assemblée reinstated it. STRASS organized counter-mobilization from 2013 onward. Founding conflict template: Socialist-driven abolitionist frame vs sex workers' union + MdM coalition.

### 2018-2020: First legal challenges and official evaluation
- **Conseil constitutionnel (QPC, 2019):** STRASS + 8 associations (including MdM) + 5 sex workers filed a QPC. February 2019, Conseil constitutionnel declared client-penalization articles "conforme" to the Constitution.
- **IGAS/IGA/IGJ mission (April 2019 to June 2020):** Authoritative bilan. Report (June 22, 2020) brutally honest: implementation "considerably slowed by a lack of political carrying," allocated credits "fluctuating and fragmented," very few public awareness measures, 25% of départements without commissions, ~230 people in exit program by June 2019. Cited by both camps ever since.
- **CEDH petition (December 2019):** 261 sex workers from various nationalities filed at ECHR, supported by ~20 associations. Simmered nearly five years.

### 2021: The minors crisis reshapes the debate
Most important inflection point. Catherine Champrenault, Procureure Générale near the Paris appeals court, delivered a report in June 2021 on minors in prostitution, tasked under the child violence plan 2020-2022. Numbers became the reference: 7,000 to 10,000 minors aged 12 to 17 estimated victims in France. Digital dimension became central (ACPE flagged "cyber-proxénétisme" and "pre-prostitutional behaviors" on social media without physical contact). Reframed debate from "adult sex workers and their clients" to "minors trapped by platforms," much harder narrative for the decriminalist camp to push back on.

Senate delegation did its own five-year review on April 8, 2021. Open letter to four ministers flagging "uneven and mitigated" application.

### 2023-2024: CEDH saga and DPTS2026 launch
- **August 2023:** CEDH unexpectedly admitted the 261 applicants' case on the merits.
- **July 2024:** CEDH ruled against the applicants. Interior Minister Aurore Bergé declared it validated France's abolitionist position. The 20-association coalition called it a "delayed application of classical jurisprudence."
- **2024:** DPTS2026 gets ANR funding (€99,426). Tullia association founded. UJM + Auvergne-Rhône-Alpes regional "Booster Innovation Pédagogique" prize adds institutional credibility.

### 2025: Political field fragments
- **Spring 2025:** Philippe Juvin (LR) deposits PPL with libertarian-conservative reformist angle.
- **October 6-10, 2025:** "Dignity by Design for Sex Workers" international transdisciplinary workshop (DPTS2026 consultation phase).
- **November 18, 2025:** LR senator Marie Mercier deposits her PPL creating an "online sexual exploitation" offense, targeting OnlyFans and Mym. Original text tried to criminalize purchase of online sex services (Swedish 2025 model). Commission des lois refused to extend prostitution offenses to digital practices, created instead an autonomous "online sexual exploitation" offense inspired by trafficking law. Schmaltz's testimony: "certainty of sanction matters more than the quantum of penalty." Commission partially absorbed the critique.
- **December 2025:** Le Monde reveals Tanguy (RN) cooperative PPL. Rossignol accuses RN of wanting to "legalize the purchase of sexual services." Political field goes from two-sided to three-sided. AIDES publishes piece flagging "deep concern in the face of a new offensive against the fundamental rights of sex workers."

### February 2026: Mercier PPL clears the Senate
- **February 10, 2026:** Senate adopted Mercier PPL 258-65 (PS against), with government support. Text rewritten during debate: original targeted consumers and intermediaries; commission version targets those who coerce others to expose themselves sexually online. Heated Rossignol vs rapporteur Josende exchanges. Mercier: "a first step: you can't do everything in one day."

**This is the most important piece for understanding where French policy is heading.** The government is not revisiting 2016; it is extending a narrower, trafficking-inspired frame to digital platforms.

### April 2026: The collision
- **April 10, 2026:** Solidaires: "Dix ans après, il est temps de passer des principes aux actes." Osez le féminisme, Mouvement du Nid, Coalition pour l'abolition in media rotation.
- **April 10-13, 2026:** Sex worker demonstrations in Paris. Bergé convenes interministerial committee on April 13 with Health Minister Stéphanie Rist. ~30 départements still without active exit program by end 2025.
- **April 13, 2026:** Senate event + AFP wire on Souyris-Kerbrat PPL (Boursorama, Orange, CNews, Focus News, Gazette France, NetAfrique, Actu Niort, Laminute, Vudailleurs). France24/AFP parallel story on police enforcement: Lénaïg Le Bail (outgoing head of OCRTEH) on online dematerialization making clients harder to identify, 95% of identified victims in enclosed settings vs 5% on street. Franceinfo runs definitive 10-year balance piece.
- **April 14, 2026:** Sciences Po academic day. Minimal direct press (specialist restitution).

## What's Actually New in This Cycle

1. **Methodology shift (legitimacy frame).** Médecins du Monde's April 2026 press release frames the community-drafted PPL as democratic/sanitary/social "exigence." The argument that sex worker expertise should drive legislative design (not policymakers' moral positions) has moved from activist talking point to mainstream press frame in ~18 months.

2. **Digital frame captured abolitionist energy.** Mercier PPL absorbed most of the abolitionist camp's 2025-2026 legislative push. Strategically significant: 2016 architecture is not being revisited; it is being treated as adequate and extended to new domains. Narrower position than Solidaires or Mouvement du Nid advocate publicly.

3. **RN entry scrambles the ideological map.** Tanguy's cooperative framing invokes "securing rights" and "choice" rhetoric, terrain historically held by the left.

4. **Belgium became the operational reference point.** Concrete enough that French reform advocates can point to a working model rather than a theoretical alternative.

## Implications for Scarlot

### 1. The "third party" criminalization is Scarlot's structural risk
Belgian reform worked precisely because it carved out the third-party economy (accountants, drivers, landlords, and by extension any service provider that enables sex work). Under French pimping doctrine, as LIEPP's Geoffroy/Le Bail/Mercat-Bruns paper documents, the offense criminalizes virtually all relationships sex workers maintain, making co-working impossible despite security benefits and discouraging police contact. **Scarlot is structurally a third-party service.** In Switzerland, where sex work is legal, this is less acute but not absent. Swiss cantonal regulation has its own "enabling" concerns, and platforms serving French clients (via Swiss hosting) could be targeted. **The Belgian carve-out list is the template Scarlot should watch.**

### 2. The Mercier PPL is the regulatory weather, not the Souyris PPL
The decriminalist PPL will generate political debate but is extremely unlikely to pass. The Mercier PPL is already on its way to the Assemblée with government backing. For a platform like Scarlot, the actual near-term risk is "online sexual exploitation" frameworks that could extend to any service provider profiting from sex workers' activity (even outside the content-subscription model). **Scarlot's positioning must clearly differentiate: tool used by sex workers, not a platform profiting from their content.**

### 3. Research legitimacy flywheel is now real
Hélène Le Bail is cited in Senate hearings, CEDH debates, mainstream press. Schmaltz cited in Mercier PPL Senate report. There are now published academic frames ("broad criminalization of pimping is harmful to the protected population") that can anchor regulator conversations, investor diligence, and product positioning. **The Geoffroy-Le Bail-Mercat-Bruns LIEPP Working Paper n°174 should be on Scarlot's reading list.** Canonical source for the structural critique.

### 4. The research validates Scarlot's premise
LIEPP working paper on pimping law is extremely relevant. The broad criminalization of "proxénétisme" is precisely the legal environment that makes tools like Scarlot both necessary and risky. Any platform that helps sex workers share information, coordinate, or vet clients risks being characterized as "facilitating prostitution" under current law.

### 5. Legislative momentum creates Swiss ripple risk
Actual PPL in the French Senate backed by a broad coalition. If France moves toward decriminalization, regulatory environment for sex worker tools shifts. Switzerland is already a different legal context (sex work legal with regulation), but French legislative debates influence the European conversation and could shape Swiss regulator thinking on platform liability and data protection.

### 6. "Community consultation" methodology is a proof point
DPTS2026 ran a structured nationwide consultation turning sex workers' needs into legal text. This validates Scarlot's user-centric, community-driven discovery approach. Scarlot's evidence tiers framework is a scrappier version of the same principle.

### 7. The collective blacklist angle has academic grounding
The event included a session on "contre-cartographie" (counter-mapping) from the community consultation. Conceptually adjacent to Scarlot's blacklist: community-sourced intelligence about dangerous clients, mapped to geographic patterns. Legitimacy framing is the same: the community, not external authorities, should control safety infrastructure.
