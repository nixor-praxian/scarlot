# Maia


---

Unknown number.

She reads the message. Polite. Direct. He says when he wants to meet.

She doesn't know who this is. She doesn't know if he's safe. She has no way to find out.

Two months ago, this same number hurt someone. The woman who was hurt told a friend. The friend posted the number in a WhatsApp group. Someone else reported it on a paid service. Someone else mentioned it in a Telegram chat in another language, in another city.

Five warnings exist. In five different places. None of them connected. None of them searchable. None of them visible to the woman reading this message right now.

She goes with her gut. She replies.

This happens every day. Everywhere. To thousands of women working alone.

---

We talked to them. Sex workers in Switzerland. Legally registered, paying taxes, running their own businesses. We didn't ask them what they wanted. We asked them how they work.

The first thing you notice is that they all solve the same problems. Independently. Without knowing each other. In completely different ways.

One renames contacts in her phone. "Bruno 24.03 14h." The name, the date, the time. That's her calendar. Her CRM. Her memory of who this person is. If she has to scroll back six months, she might find it. She might not.

Another went further. She invented a language. Emoji codes next to each name. A credit card means he pays on time. A red circle means danger. A word she made up, "fantasmeur," means he talks but never shows up. She built a classification system inside WhatsApp using stickers and labels, because nothing else existed.

A third keeps her entire client history on an old phone she no longer uses for anything else. It sits in a drawer. If she loses it, years of knowledge disappear.

Every one of them built something. None of them built the same thing. And all of it is fragile. One phone. One app. One person's memory holding it together.

They are all, independently, building the same product. Piece by piece. Alone.

One of them said that. My own shop. My own rules. My own tools.

She was describing, without knowing it, exactly what we're building.

There are an estimated 40 million sex workers in the world. In Switzerland, the profession has been legal since 1942. Registered. Taxed. Regulated.

We know this industry. We've been inside it for months.

We've identified **6'425 workers** active on Swiss platforms today. Not an estimate, an account of the ones that are visible online on 7 platforms, monitored continuously for three weeks, profiles matched by phone number. Approximately **547 in Geneva.** **1,238 in French-speaking Switzerland. **17% maintain profiles on two or more platforms simultaneously - they actively invest in reaching clients across multiple channels.

We know where they work. Geneva is the largest market, followed by Lausanne, Basel, Bern, and Zurich. We know their tools. WhatsApp is dominant. We know what they pay for. And we know what they can't find at any price: a professional tool built for what they actually do.

The problems they face are not unique to their profession. Any professional who works alone with clients faces the same ones. A masseuse. A therapist. A tutor. A nanny.

The difference is that for sex workers, the stakes are higher. And nobody is building anything for them.

## What we're building

A personal AI agent that lives where you already communicate with your clients. It manages your client relationships through conversation. No new app to download. No new interface to learn.

We call it **Maia** *(codename, to be confirmed between founders)* - after the Greek Titan who gave birth to Hermes, the messenger god. She nurtures what the messenger carries. The name fits: Maia doesn't replace the conversation. She nurtures the relationship around it.

Maia is modular. Users pick **coverages** - capability bundles - based on what they need:

- **Safety**: Inbound screening, blacklist lookup, risk flagging, collective warnings
- **Memory**: Client CRM - who, when, preferences, notes, history
- **Booking**: Availability, appointments, reminders, no-show tracking
- **Payments**: Deposits, cancellation fees, escrow, receipts
- **Reputation**: Collective client database, shared warnings across users

Different service providers combine different coverages. A sex worker picks Safety + Memory + Reputation. A masseuse picks Memory + Booking + Payments. Same engine. Different configuration.

We start with the hardest vertical - sex workers - because:
- The need is most acute (safety is existential, not just convenient)
- We have insider access (our co-founder has deep community trust in Geneva)
- We have numerous validated interviews confirming the top 3 problems
- Zero competition for attention - no one is building for this population
- **If it works for the hardest use case, it works for everyone**

## Why now

Three things changed:

**1. AI agents are good enough.** Two years ago, a conversational AI that could screen messages, remember client history, and take actions on your behalf was science fiction. Today it's a weekend project. The technology caught up to the need.

**2. Decriminalization is accelerating.** Australia decriminalized in Victoria (2023) and Queensland (2024). Belgium granted full labor rights to sex workers (2022). Each new decriminalized jurisdiction is a new market where a tool like this can operate openly.

**3. Every platform that tried to serve this population got killed** - by payment processors, by app stores, or by FOSTA-SESTA. Switter had 430,000 users and shut down. Backpage was seized by the FBI. ClientEye appears defunct. The space is a graveyard of platforms that tried to be marketplaces. **Nobody has tried being a personal tool.**

Maia is not a marketplace. It does not connect buyers and sellers. It does not advertise services. It does not process payments between clients and providers. It is a personal AI agent that runs on the provider's own phone, managing their own contacts. The legal analogy is not Backpage - it is a CRM.
