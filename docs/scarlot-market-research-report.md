# Sex Work Industry: Market Size, Legal Landscape, and Technology Ecosystem

*Deep Research Report - Scarlot Business Plan Foundation*
*Date: 2 April 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

The global sex work industry generates an estimated $100–186 billion annually, though all figures carry significant uncertainty due to the clandestine nature of much of the activity. Europe alone is estimated to have 700,000–1 million sex workers, with Germany (~400,000), the Netherlands (~25,000), and Switzerland (~20,000–30,000) as the largest regulated markets. Switzerland's fully legal framework - in place since 1942, regulated at the cantonal level - makes it one of the most favorable jurisdictions for a platform business serving independent sex workers.

The technology ecosystem serving sex workers is remarkably underdeveloped. Existing platforms fall into three categories: listing/advertising sites (Tryst, F-Girl, And6), safety/blacklist tools (National Ugly Mugs, ClientEye, CheckPunter), and adjacent platforms (OnlyFans). No integrated solution exists that combines CRM, safety screening, and blacklist functionality in a single product. Every platform that has attempted to serve this population has faced one of three killers: payment processor hostility, app store policy enforcement, or FOSTA-SESTA-style legal exposure.

For Scarlot's business plan, we estimate a TAM of ~€1.2–2.4 billion (safety + CRM tools for sex workers in legalized/decriminalized markets globally), a SAM of ~€50–120 million (independent workers in Western Europe's regulated markets), and a SOM of ~€2–5 million (Switzerland + Geneva-adjacent French-speaking markets in year 1–3). These estimates are constructed bottom-up and should be treated as directional, not precise.

**Critical caveat on all data in this report:** Sex work statistics are notoriously unreliable. Estimates vary by 5–10x depending on source, methodology, and political orientation. We flag credibility for every number cited.

## 1. Global Market Size

### The Problem with Numbers

There is no authoritative, consensus figure for the global sex work market. Every estimate must be understood in context of who produced it, when, and with what methodology.

**Commonly cited figures:**

| Estimate                                                                 | Source                                           | Credibility | Notes                                                                                        |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------ | ----------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $186 billion global revenue                                              | Havocscope (aggregator of informal economy data) | LOW–MEDIUM  | Widely cited but methodology opaque; aggregates disparate national estimates [1]             |
| ~1% of global GDP (~$1 trillion)                                         | Various media citations                          | LOW         | Round-number estimate; likely includes trafficking, pornography, and adjacent industries [1] |
| $99 billion in annual profits from forced commercial sexual exploitation | ILO (2014)                                       | MEDIUM–HIGH | Credible source but covers FORCED exploitation only, not consensual sex work [2]             |
| $173 billion from forced commercial sexual exploitation                  | ILO (2024 update)                                | HIGH        | Updated methodology; 6.3 million people in forced sexual exploitation globally [3]           |
| 40–42 million sex workers globally                                       | Fondation Scelles (2012)                         | MEDIUM      | Most-cited global headcount; dated; includes all forms of sex work [4]                       |

**Our assessment:** A reasonable range for the global *consensual* sex work market is **$100–200 billion** in annual revenue. This excludes trafficking, pornography, and digital-only platforms like OnlyFans. The ILO's $173 billion figure for forced exploitation alone suggests the total market (forced + consensual) is substantially larger, but conflating these categories would be misleading and ethically inappropriate.

### OnlyFans as a Market Signal

OnlyFans provides the most transparent data point in the adjacent digital sex work economy [5]:

- **$7.22 billion** gross revenue in fiscal 2024 (up 9% YoY)
- **$5.80 billion** paid to creators
- **4.63 million** creator accounts (up 13%)
- **377.5 million** fan accounts (up 24%)

While OnlyFans is digital-only and not directly comparable to in-person sex work, it demonstrates the scale of willingness to pay for sexual services when friction is reduced. The average creator earns just $131/month, with extreme concentration at the top (0.1% of creators earn ~76% of revenue).

## 2. European Market

### EU National Accounts: Prostitution in GDP

Since 2014, under ESA 2010 accounting standards, EU member states are required to include estimates of prostitution services in their GDP calculations - but only where both parties are willing participants [6]. This creates the unusual situation where official government statisticians must estimate the size of an industry that is often only partially legal.

**Country-level estimates:**

| Country | Est. Workers | Market Size | GDP Contribution | Source Credibility |
|---------|-------------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Germany | ~400,000 (est.) / 45,000 registered (2022) | ~€14.6 billion/year | ~0.4% GDP | Workers: LOW (widely repeated, origin unclear). Revenue: MEDIUM (Destatis methodology). Registration gap (400k vs 45k) reveals the estimation problem [7] |
| Netherlands | 20,000–30,000 | ~€800 million/year | <0.1% GDP | MEDIUM - Dutch statistical office estimates [8] |
| Spain | ~100,000–300,000 | Not reliably estimated | Included in GDP since 2014 | LOW - range too wide to be useful |
| France | ~30,000–40,000 (pre-2016) | Not reliably estimated | Nordic model since 2016 | MEDIUM - Fondation Scelles/government estimates |
| Italy | ~70,000–120,000 | Included in GDP since 2014 | ~0.1% GDP | LOW–MEDIUM |
| EU total | ~700,000–1,000,000 | Tens of billions EUR | Variable | LOW - aggregation of unreliable national estimates |

**The academic estimate:** Adair & Nezhyvenko (2017), in a peer-reviewed paper presented at OECD, attempted to systematically estimate the EU prostitution market using an ordered probit model [9]. Their methodology is the most rigorous available, though still dependent on underlying national estimates.

### The Germany Case Study

Germany is the largest regulated sex work market in Europe and provides the most instructive comparison for Switzerland [7]:

- **Prostitution Act (2002):** Legalized sex work as a legitimate profession. Workers could obtain employment contracts, social insurance, and legal protections.
- **Prostitutes Protection Act (2017):** Required individual registration with local authorities, mandatory health counseling, and registration certificates (valid 2 years, 1 year if under 21).
- **Registration gap:** Of ~400,000 estimated workers, only ~45,000 were registered by 2022. This 89% non-compliance rate is the central lesson: **legalization without low-friction implementation fails to reach the population it aims to serve.**
- **Revenue:** €14.6 billion annually is the most-cited figure, making Germany's sex industry roughly the size of its steel industry.
- **Current debate:** Germany is actively discussing rolling back legalization amid exploitation concerns, which could reduce the largest regulated market in Europe.

### The France Case Study (Nordic Model)

France adopted the Nordic model in April 2016, criminalizing the purchase (but not the sale) of sex [10]. Post-implementation research shows:

- 63% of sex workers reported deteriorating living conditions
- 42% reported increased exposure to violence
- 38% found it harder to negotiate condom use
- Violence incidents in the Bois de Boulogne doubled (8 in 2016 → 18 in first 9 months of 2017)
- Only ~4,500 fines issued in 5 years - minimal enforcement

**Implication for Scarlot:** France's Nordic model makes it a hostile market for any platform serving sex workers. However, French-speaking sex workers operating from Switzerland (a legal jurisdiction) represent an accessible market segment - and several of Scarlot's interviewees operate across this border.

## 3. Switzerland: The Home Market

### Legal Framework

Switzerland has one of the world's most established legal frameworks for sex work [11] [12]:

- **Legal since 1942** - among the longest-standing legalization regimes globally
- **Constitutional basis:** Sex work is a permitted gainful activity under economic freedom principles
- **Federal minimum rules:** Consensual exchange between adults; worker must be self-employed without coercion
- **Federal criminal offenses:** Encouraging prostitution of minors or through coercion (Art. 195, up to 10 years); unauthorized practice violating cantonal rules (Art. 199, fines); human trafficking (Art. 182)

### Cantonal Regulation

Switzerland's federal structure means 26 cantons can each regulate sex work differently [11] [12]:

- **10 of 26 cantons** have adopted specific sex work legislation (as of 2022)
- Cantons with specific legislation: Ticino, Geneva, Vaud, Fribourg, Neuchatel, Jura, and others
- Requirements vary: registration, police checks, health monitoring, zoning restrictions
- **Geneva** has specific prostitution legislation with authorized locations (Les Pâquis)
- Red-light districts exist in Zurich (Langstrasse), Bern (Lorraine), Geneva (Les Pâquis), Lausanne (Sevelin), Basel (Kleinbasel), Lugano (Loreto)

### Market Size

| Metric | Estimate | Source | Credibility |
|--------|----------|-------|-------------|
| Total sex workers | 20,000–30,000 | UNAIDS / various | MEDIUM - consistent across sources but methodology unclear |
| Street-based workers | ~10% of total | Swiss research | MEDIUM |
| Indoor/online | ~90% of total | Swiss research | MEDIUM |
| Zurich street workers | ~5,000 | Local estimates | LOW - single source |

**Revenue estimate:** If we assume 20,000–30,000 workers with average annual revenue of CHF 50,000–80,000 (a rough range based on interview data and comparative markets), the Swiss sex work market is approximately **CHF 1–2.4 billion annually.** This is a constructed estimate, not an official figure.

**Proprietary data (Scarlot market census, March–April 2026):** Continuous scraping of 7 Swiss platforms over 3 weeks identified **5,229 unique, phone-deduplicated workers** with **9,091 active listings**. This represents the digitally-visible, platform-advertised segment - the actual total (including street-based, word-of-mouth-only, and agency workers not on these platforms) is larger. Of these, **1,238 are in French-speaking Switzerland** (the launch market) and **547 are in Geneva alone**. **17.6% are active on 2+ platforms simultaneously**, suggesting higher engagement and tool sophistication. (Note: premium/featured placement detection is not yet reliable across all platforms - only 2 of 7 adapters implement it, and xdate marks 100% of listings as "premium" by default. This metric needs a per-platform audit before citing.)

### Work Permits and Access

The 90-day permit system is a critical structural feature [12]:
- EU/EFTA nationals can work for up to 90 days per calendar year without a residence permit
- This creates a transient population of sex workers - and a vulnerability window (confirmed by Scarlot's INT8 interview: "90 day permit - financial pressure, taking advantage of that")
- Non-EU workers require standard work permits, which are difficult to obtain

## 4. Legal Status: Global Map

### The Four Models

| Model | How it works | Countries | Platform implications |
|-------|-------------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Full legalization** | Sex work is a regulated profession | Germany, Netherlands, Switzerland, Austria, parts of Australia, Nevada (US) | FAVORABLE - platforms can operate openly; payment processing still challenging |
| **Decriminalization** | Criminal penalties removed; regulated as any work | New Zealand (2003), NSW Australia (1995), Victoria (2023), Queensland (2024) | MOST FAVORABLE - least legal risk for platforms; NZ is the gold standard |
| **Nordic/Equality model** | Selling legal, buying criminalized | Sweden (1999), Norway, Iceland, Canada (2014), France (2016), Ireland (2017), Israel | HOSTILE - platforms enabling buyer-seller connection risk facilitating a crime |
| **Criminalization** | All aspects illegal | Most US states, most of Africa, most of Asia, Russia | NOT VIABLE - platform operation is illegal |

### European Legal Map (2026)

**Legal/Regulated:** Germany, Netherlands, Switzerland, Austria, Greece, Hungary, Latvia, Turkey
**Decriminalized (partially):** Belgium, Czech Republic, Denmark, Finland, Italy, Luxembourg, Poland, Portugal, Spain, UK (England/Wales/Scotland)
**Nordic model:** Sweden, Norway, Iceland, France, Ireland, Northern Ireland
**Ambiguous/restrictive:** Romania, Bulgaria, Lithuania, Croatia

### Recent Trends (2023–2026)

- **Australia** is the major mover: Victoria decriminalized (December 2023), Queensland followed (August 2024). Australia is now largely decriminalized.
- **Germany** is debating rolling back legalization amid trafficking concerns - potential major market disruption
- **EU Parliament** has debated but not adopted an EU-wide Nordic model resolution
- **US:** FOSTA-SESTA remains in force; SAFE SEX Workers Study Act reintroduced (December 2024) to study repeal impacts
- No major European countries changed their legal status in 2025–2026

### Why Platforms Fail in This Space

Every platform serving sex workers faces the same three killers:

1. **Payment processor hostility:** Visa, Mastercard, PayPal, and Stripe all have explicit policies against sex work-related services. Even where legal, processors refuse to serve the industry. OnlyFans nearly banned explicit content in 2021 under Mastercard pressure.

2. **App store enforcement:** Google Play and Apple App Store policies restrict sex work-related apps. National Ugly Mugs faced Google policy changes threatening its safety app [16]. This forces web-first or sideloaded distribution.

3. **FOSTA-SESTA exposure (US):** Any platform that can be construed as "facilitating" sex work faces legal liability in the US, even if operating in jurisdictions where sex work is legal. This killed Switter (430,000+ users, shut down due to compliance impossibility) [14] and chilled investment across the sector.

**Switter's death is the cautionary tale:** Built by Assembly Four (Australia), launched 2018, reached 430,000+ users, provided Mastodon-based social networking + bad date lists + safety tools. Shut down because Australian, UK, and US legislative changes made compliance impossible across jurisdictions [14]. The lesson: **jurisdiction selection is existential.**

### The Gap Scarlot Fills

No existing tool combines:
- Client blacklist (personal + collective)
- CRM/client memory
- Inbound screening/triage
- Conversational interface through existing messaging apps
- Focus on independent workers (not agencies)

The landscape is fragmented across three models: (1) NGO alert systems (NUM, Ugly Mugs AU/NZ, Canadian Bad Date Lists, Projet Jasmine) -- free, safety-focused, but analog and distribution-based; (2) commercial verification (P411, BMG, Date-Check) -- paid, digital, but US-centric or siloed; (3) informal networks (WhatsApp groups, Telegram bots, forum threads on Ladies.de/Kaufmich) -- free, real-time, but unstructured and trust-dependent.

National Ugly Mugs is the closest in mission (safety) but is a reporting system, not a CRM. P411 is the closest commercial model (subscription screening) but is US-only, vouch-based, and FOSTA-disrupted. Projet Jasmine is the closest in lookup functionality (phone/email/plate search with tiered classification) but is France-only and not conversational. BMG is the closest in market (Swiss escorts) but is expensive and siloed. ProCoRe's planned 2027 launch is the most significant Swiss competitive signal -- but is collective-only, restricted to criminal offenses, and web-based (not conversational).

See `docs/safety-lookup-landscape.md` for the full competitive catalog.

## 6. TAM / SAM / SOM

### Methodology

We use a bottom-up approach based on worker counts and estimated willingness to pay, cross-referenced against the lone worker safety solutions market as an adjacent comparable.

### Adjacent Market: Lone Worker Safety Solutions

The lone worker safety solutions market provides the closest B2B SaaS comparison [20]:
- **Market size:** ~$1.2–1.8 billion (2024), projected to reach $4–6 billion by 2033
- **CAGR:** 8–14% depending on source
- **Key players:** StaySafe (100,000+ users), GetHomeSafe (400+ organizations, $3.85/user/mo)
- **Pricing:** $3–15/user/month for SaaS platforms

Sex workers are, by definition, lone workers operating in high-risk environments. The safety tool value proposition is identical.

### TAM: Total Addressable Market

**All independent sex workers in countries where sex work is legal or decriminalized, who could benefit from a safety + CRM tool.**

| Market | Est. Independent Workers | WTP/worker/year (est.) | TAM |
|--------|------------------------|----------------------|-----|
| Germany | ~200,000 independent (50% of 400k est.) | €120–240 | €24–48M |
| Netherlands | ~15,000 independent | €120–240 | €1.8–3.6M |
| Switzerland | ~15,000 independent | CHF 120–600 | CHF 1.8–9M |
| UK (decrim regions) | ~50,000 independent | £120–240 | £6–12M |
| Australia/NZ | ~30,000 independent | A$120–240 | A$3.6–7.2M |
| Rest of EU (legal/decrim) | ~100,000 independent | €120–240 | €12–24M |
| **Global legalized markets** | **~500,000–700,000** | **€120–240** | **€60–170M** |

Including adjacent digital sex workers (OnlyFans creators who also do in-person work, cam models who meet clients), the TAM could extend to **€200M–500M+** for a comprehensive platform.

**Conservative TAM: ~€100–250 million.** This is the market for a SaaS safety + CRM tool across all regulated markets.

## 7. Key Trends

### 1. Digitization and the Shift to Independence

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated a structural shift that was already underway [21]:
- OnlyFans grew from 7.5 million users (Nov 2019) to 120 million (March 2021)
- In-person sex workers migrated to digital platforms during lockdowns
- Post-pandemic, many maintained hybrid models (online + in-person)
- The independent worker percentage has grown relative to agency models, enabled by digital advertising and direct client communication

### 2. Platform Deplatforming and Payment Hostility

FOSTA-SESTA (2018) created a chilling effect that extends far beyond the US [15]:
- Backpage seizure (2018) destroyed the dominant advertising platform
- Switter shutdown (2022) proved even non-US platforms can't escape the compliance problem [14]
- Social media platforms systematically deplatform sex workers (Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok)
- Payment processors (Visa, Mastercard, PayPal) refuse sex work-related businesses
- Bank account closures are common and unexpected

**Implication for Scarlot:** Operating in Switzerland (fully legal) with a WhatsApp-based architecture avoids app store dependency. Payment processing remains the critical infrastructure risk.

### 3. Decriminalization Momentum

The global trend is toward decriminalization, not criminalization [22]:
- Australia: Victoria (2023) and Queensland (2024) decriminalized
- New Zealand: 20+ years of successful decriminalization
- US: Growing advocacy for repeal of FOSTA-SESTA
- Amnesty International, WHO, UNAIDS, Human Rights Watch all advocate for decriminalization

Each new decriminalized jurisdiction is a potential new market for platform tools.

### 4. Safety as the Unmet Need

Across all regulatory models, safety remains the primary unmet need:
- National Ugly Mugs processes ~1 million safety alerts/year in the UK alone [16]
- Scarlot's own research: P3 (no inbound filtering) and P5 (no centralized blacklist) are the top-priority problems across 8 interviews
- No existing tool adequately addresses safety for independent workers in a non-cumbersome way

### 5. Data Sovereignty Concerns

Sex workers have heightened concerns about data control (confirmed by Scarlot INT8):
- Platform account suspension risk (Notion, social media)
- Bank account closure without warning
- Data used against workers in legal proceedings
- GDPR/nFADP implications for sensitive personal data

## 10. Risk Analysis

### 10.1 Platform Dependency Risk: WhatsApp / Meta

**Severity: HIGH - this is the most critical risk in the business plan.**

WhatsApp is owned by Meta, a US company. This creates multiple risk vectors:

**WhatsApp Business Policy explicitly prohibits:**
- "Selling, buying, promoting, or facilitating the exchange of adult services"
- "Operating, buying, selling, promoting, or otherwise facilitating the exchange of illegal products or services"
- "Messages containing sexually explicit materials or nudity"

Source: [WhatsApp Business Policy](https://business.whatsapp.com/policy)

**The Baileys (unofficial API) risk:**
- Scarlot uses Baileys, an unofficial WhatsApp Web API that connects via the Linked Devices feature
- WhatsApp actively detects and bans accounts using unofficial APIs
- GitHub issue #1869 on WhiskeySockets/Baileys reports "high number of bans"
- Reconnect loops can trigger ban detection systems
- There is NO legitimate, ToS-compliant way to automate WhatsApp for sex work-related services

**What this means:** Scarlot's entire current architecture depends on a platform that (a) is owned by a US company subject to FOSTA-SESTA, (b) explicitly prohibits the use case in its ToS, and (c) actively bans accounts using the API Scarlot relies on. This is not a theoretical risk - it is a near-certainty that accounts will be disrupted.

**Mitigation strategy:**
1. **Multi-channel architecture from day one.** WhatsApp as initial channel, but the system must be channel-agnostic. Telegram (more permissive ToS, Dubai-based) should be a parallel option. Matrix/Element (self-hosted, open protocol) as the long-term resilient channel.
2. **Channel abstraction layer.** The bot logic must be decoupled from the transport layer. Switching from WhatsApp to Telegram should be a configuration change, not a rewrite.
3. **User education.** TDS must understand that WhatsApp accounts may be suspended. The onboarding should include backup channel setup.
4. **No explicit content through the bot.** The bot handles safety checks and CRM - never explicit content. This minimizes (but does not eliminate) ToS risk.

### 10.2 FOSTA-SESTA Exposure

**Severity: MEDIUM - real but manageable for a Swiss entity.**

FOSTA-SESTA (2018) amends Section 230 of the US Communications Decency Act. A platform can be held liable if it acts with "intent to promote or facilitate" prostitution, or if it "knowingly" assists sex trafficking.

**Does FOSTA-SESTA reach Scarlot?**

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| Company incorporated in Switzerland | Reduces but does not eliminate US jurisdictional reach |
| No US servers, no US users | Significantly reduces exposure |
| Uses WhatsApp (US company) as transport | Creates a potential US nexus through Meta |
| Uses Anthropic (US company) as AI provider | Creates a potential US nexus |
| Founders travel to US | Personal jurisdiction risk if founders enter US |
| No US payment processing | Reduces exposure |

**US extraterritorial enforcement precedent:** The US has successfully prosecuted and fined Swiss companies (Alstom Network Schweiz AG, SITA) for violations of US law. The mechanism is usually: (a) US resources were used (US dollars, US financial system, US email servers), or (b) US persons or companies were involved.

**Key legal question:** Using WhatsApp (Meta) and Claude (Anthropic) means Scarlot's data flows through US-owned infrastructure. This could be argued as using "US resources," creating a jurisdictional hook.

**Mitigation:**
1. Legal opinion from Swiss counsel on FOSTA-SESTA extraterritorial reach to a Swiss entity
2. Minimize US nexus: avoid US payment processors, US hosting, US banking
3. Consider whether Anthropic's Claude API creates unacceptable exposure (evaluate European AI alternatives)
4. Founders should be advised on personal travel to US - though prosecution of a Swiss safety tool is extremely unlikely, it is not zero

### 10.3 The "Facilitating" Question - Is an AI Agent a Platform?

**This is the strongest argument in Scarlot's defense.**

FOSTA-SESTA targets platforms that "facilitate" the connection between buyers and sellers of sex. The law was written for platforms like Backpage that hosted user-generated advertisements connecting clients with sex workers.

**What Scarlot's Claw actually does:**
- Screens incoming messages against a personal blacklist
- Stores client notes (CRM) on the TDS's own device
- Runs as a linked device on the TDS's own WhatsApp number
- Does NOT connect buyers and sellers
- Does NOT advertise sexual services
- Does NOT process payments
- Does NOT host user-generated content
- Does NOT enable discovery (clients find the TDS through other channels)

**The legal analogy is not "platform" - it is "personal productivity tool."**

A CRM does not facilitate the underlying business any more than:
- A spreadsheet used by a sex worker to track clients "facilitates" prostitution
- A calendar app used to schedule appointments "facilitates" prostitution
- A contact management app used to store notes "facilitates" prostitution
- A safety app like National Ugly Mugs "facilitates" prostitution

**The critical distinction:** Scarlot does not create a marketplace. It does not bring buyers and sellers together. It operates AFTER the client has already contacted the TDS through independent channels. It is a post-contact screening and record-keeping tool.

**The blacklist dimension:** The collective blacklist feature (C-01) is the most legally sensitive component. Sharing flagged client data between users moves Scarlot from "personal tool" toward "network." However, National Ugly Mugs in the UK operates a collective warning system legally - the precedent exists, in a jurisdiction where selling sex is legal.

**Bottom line:** The AI agent framing strengthens the legal position. Maia is a personal assistant running on the user's own device, processing the user's own contacts, using the user's own number. It is closer to Siri or Google Assistant than to Backpage.

### 10.5 Data and Privacy Risk (nFADP)

**Severity: HIGH - this is a legal requirement, not optional.**

The Swiss new Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP, effective September 2023) classifies data about sexual behavior and orientation as **sensitive personal data** requiring enhanced protection.

Scarlot processes:
- Client phone numbers (personal data)
- Client behavioral notes (potentially sensitive)
- Blacklist flags (highly sensitive - allegations of criminal behavior)
- Meeting records (sexual behavior data under nFADP)

**Requirements:**
- Explicit consent for processing sensitive data
- Data minimization principle
- Right to deletion
- Data breach notification (72 hours)
- Potential requirement for Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)
- Cross-border data transfer restrictions

**Architecture implications:**
- Data should stay on-device where possible (strengthens both privacy and legal position)
- Cloud-synced blacklist data requires the highest level of protection
- Consider federated architecture for collective blacklist (data never centralized)
- Anonymization / pseudonymization for shared data

## 11. Strategic Positioning: Maia Platform

### The Reframe

Scarlot should not be positioned as a "sex work platform." It should be positioned as a **coverage pack** on a generic AI agent platform for independent service providers.

The core insight: every independent service provider - sex worker, masseuse, therapist, tutor, nanny - faces the same operational friction. Who is this person? Have I seen them before? When am I available? Did they pay? Maia is a personal AI agent that solves these through conversation on the provider's existing messaging app.

### Coverages (Modular Capability Bundles)

| Coverage | What it does | Universal? |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Safety** | Inbound screening, blacklist lookup, risk flagging, collective warnings | Sex workers, home-visit therapists, nannies, tutors |
| **Memory** | Client CRM - who, when, preferences, notes, grades, history | All verticals |
| **Booking** | Availability, appointments, reminders, no-show tracking | All verticals |
| **Payments** | Deposit collection, cancellation fees, escrow, receipt generation | All verticals |
| **Switchboard** | Inbound routing, auto-reply, availability broadcast | High-volume providers |
| **Reputation** | Collective client database, shared warnings | Sex workers, gig workers |
| **Compliance** | Income logging, tax categorization, expense tracking | Registered independents |
| **Identity** | Self-controlled profile, broadcast channels, direct marketing | Anyone leaving listings |

**Scarlot = Safety + Memory + Reputation + Booking.** It is the first coverage pack, not the company.

### How This Changes the Risk Profile

| Risk | "Scarlot" (sex work company) | "Claw Platform" (service provider tool) |
|------|----------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| Payment processing | BLOCKED | POSSIBLE - generic service marketplace |
| App store approval | BLOCKED | POSSIBLE - personal service assistant |
| FOSTA-SESTA exposure | MEDIUM | LOW - generic tool, vertical-agnostic |
| Investor framing | Niche, stigma | "Doctolib for independents" |
| Founder reputation | Sex work company | Service platform founder |
| Marketing channels | Restricted | Standard B2B SaaS |

### The "Facilitating" Defense, Strengthened

Under the platform model, Maia is not just "not facilitating sex work" - it literally doesn't know what vertical it's serving. The same engine handles a TDS client screening and a masseuse appointment reminder. The coverage configuration is the user's choice, not the platform's intent.

This is the same reason Stripe isn't liable for what its merchants sell, and Calendly isn't liable for what appointments its users schedule.

### The Fine-Tuning Flywheel

1. **Individual:** Each Claw learns its owner's patterns through conversation
2. **Vertical:** Aggregated anonymous patterns improve coverage packs (no-show prediction, risk signals)
3. **Cross-vertical:** Patterns transfer - no-show behavior, payment friction, screening heuristics are universal

The defensibility isn't the code (NanoClaw is open source). It's the accumulated behavioral intelligence across verticals, trained by real users.

## 12. Recommendations for the Business Plan

1. **Position as a platform, not a product.** "AI agent infrastructure for independent service providers." Scarlot is the first coverage pack. Sex workers are the first vertical because the need is most acute and the co-founder has community access.

2. **Build channel-agnostic from day one.** Maia meets users where they already communicate - but it must survive any single channel dying. Technology, channels, and interfaces are implementation details driven by market needs. Frame this honestly: "architected to survive platform eviction," not "avoids platform risk."

3. **Lead with Switzerland's regulatory advantage.** Legal since 1942. The home market for proving the model - but the infrastructure is jurisdiction-agnostic.

4. **Use the adjacent market framing.** Lone worker safety solutions ($1.2–1.8B) is the investor anchor. "Same value proposition, same user profile (lone workers in high-risk environments), delivered through conversational AI instead of a standalone app."

5. **Be honest about data uncertainty and risks.** The risk section should be prominent, not buried. WhatsApp dependency, FOSTA-SESTA reach, nFADP compliance - these are known risks with mitigation plans, not surprises waiting to happen.

6. **The "facilitating" defense is strong - lean into it.** Maia is a personal AI agent. It does not create a marketplace. It operates post-contact. The legal analogy is productivity tool, not Backpage. Under the platform model, it doesn't even know what vertical it's serving.

7. **Expansion = new coverage packs, not new markets.** The second vertical (therapists? trainers?) validates the platform hypothesis. If the same engine serves two different verticals, the platform thesis is proven.

8. **Start SOM small and honest.** CHF 200K–1M Year 3 ARR in FR-CH. The growth story is vertical expansion (sex work → therapy → training → ...), not geographic expansion.
