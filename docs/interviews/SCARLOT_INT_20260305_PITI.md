## 07. Contradictions & Surprises

**SURPRISE-1: Blocks but doesn't record - yet participates in oral blacklist.**
On the boulevard, Piti actively shares information about dangerous individuals and cars. But online, she blocks and moves on with no record-keeping. The contradiction is not one of values but of infrastructure: the boulevard provides a social layer (colleagues physically present) that makes sharing frictionless. Online work has no equivalent. This reinforces that the digital blacklist gap is an infrastructure problem, not a behavior problem.

**SURPRISE-2: The "perdus" category reframes P3 (no inbound filtering).**
Previous interviews framed inbound filtering as a binary problem: wanted vs. unwanted contacts. Piti's taxonomy introduces a third category - contacts who are unwanted in the moment but are genuinely interested and might convert. This complicates a pure blocking/filtering approach. An ideal triage system might need a "slow lane" for hesitant contacts rather than a binary accept/reject.

**SURPRISE-3: Financial pressure increases both labor and risk.**
Counterintuitively, when Piti is financially pressured, she does MORE screening work (internet research, photo lookup, linguistic analysis) but also takes MORE risk (she doesn't flee at the first doubt). The screening labor increases because she can't afford to lose the potential income, but the risk tolerance increases for the same reason. A tool that makes screening fast and free could break this dynamic.

**SURPRISE-4: Hotel cost absorbed as safety tax.**
Piti pays for hotel rooms herself to control the encounter location. If the client no-shows, she absorbs the cost. This is a direct financial penalty for maintaining safety. No previous interview has quantified a monetary safety cost so concretely.

## 13. Researcher Notes & Bias Flags

**METHODOLOGICAL NOTES:**

1. **Written questionnaire limitations.** This was a self-administered written questionnaire, not a live interview. This means:
   - No follow-up probing was possible in real time
   - The depth of each answer was determined by Piti's writing inclination, not by interviewer skill
   - Emotional intensity and body language cues are absent
   - The Mom Test principle (no leading, no pitching) was structurally enforced by the format, which is a strength
   - However, the questions themselves may have contained framing that influenced responses (e.g., "ce que tu appelles des indésirables" in Q1 suggests the questionnaire introduced the term)

2. **Questionnaire framing.** Piti's use of "ce que tu appelles des indésirables" in Q1 indicates the questionnaire used this term first. Her 3-category taxonomy was her own elaboration, but the umbrella concept was seeded by the question. This slightly reduces the "unprompted" quality of the signal, though the taxonomy itself remains unprompted and original.

3. **Small sample context.** This is interview 4. Patterns are emerging (blocking as universal workaround, absence of digital blacklist) but the sample is still small. Dual-mode and trans perspectives are now represented for the first time, enriching diversity.

**BIAS FLAGS:**

- **Confirmation bias risk (boulevard blacklist).** The boulevard oral blacklist perfectly validates A1 (willingness to share) and P5 (no digital blacklist). This is a strong signal, but the researcher should be cautious about over-generalizing from street-work culture to digital-work culture. Boulevard sharing may be driven by physical proximity and immediate danger rather than by a transferable trust model.

- **Novelty bias risk (virtual panic button).** The panic button request is exciting because it is spontaneous and novel. But it is a single mention from a single interviewee, grounded in a specific venue context (Barillon). It should not be elevated to a priority feature without validation from other interviewees.

- **Survivorship bias.** Piti describes herself as pragmatic and resilient ("ce n'est pas dans mon tempérament de me prendre la tête"). She has developed effective workarounds over years. TDS who were less resilient or who experienced worse outcomes may have left the profession. Piti's perspective, while valuable, may understate the severity of the problems for less experienced or less resourceful workers.

- **Trans-specific experiences may be under-explored.** The questionnaire does not appear to have asked about trans-specific risks (targeting, fetishization, discrimination). Piti's experiences of violence and stealthing may have trans-specific dimensions that were not surfaced. A follow-up should explore whether her threat landscape differs from cis TDS in ways relevant to tool design.

- **Financial precarity framing.** Piti volunteered this insight, but the researcher should avoid assuming all TDS experience the same precarity-safety tradeoff. Some may have stable income and still take risks; others may be precarious and still refuse all risky contacts. The structural insight is valid but should be validated as a pattern, not assumed as universal.
