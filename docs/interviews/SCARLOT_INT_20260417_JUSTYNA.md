## 07. Contradictions & Surprises

**What was expected:** A low-volume worker would report low cognitive overload (P4) and low filtering pain (P3).
**What was found:** P3 (no inbound filtering) and P4 (cognitive overload) are felt as intensely as in high-volume interviewees. Mental toll is volume-independent.
**Possible explanations:** Pain scales with inbound noise, not with converted sessions. Filtering is 100% of the cognitive labor regardless of booking count. Low volume actually intensifies the signal-to-noise ratio.
**What to investigate next:** On the next interview, ask directly: "If inbound noise disappeared tomorrow, how many more clients would you take?" to test whether the inbound-filter is the active limiter on supply.

**What was expected:** BMG-group usage is niche and stigmatised within the community.
**What was found:** JUSTYNA normalises it completely: "without this check it's impossible to work." Framed as mainstream professional practice.
**Possible explanations:** Convergent with MERON and GABRIELLE; the stigma hypothesis is weakening. The BMG community layer is more prevalent than the advisor hypothesised.
**What to investigate next:** Quantify — what share of BMG subscribers are in the group? Is group access the real ROI on the subscription?

**What was expected:** Interviewee would differentiate between personal and collective blacklist needs.
**What was found:** She treats them as one thing. Personal block (iPhone) and collective post (WhatsApp group) are both responses to the same event, separated only by whether the offense is severe enough to share.
**Possible explanations:** Validates Scarlot's structural insight — the CRM and blacklist are one data model; proportionality is the user-controlled variable. Matches Gabrielle's INT6 finding.
**What to investigate next:** Test whether a single "mark this contact" action with a severity dial (private → local community → broader) is intuitive on the next user.

## 12. Recommended Next Actions

### For the interviewer

**What went well:** Strong existing rapport — the interviewee opened up freely about blocking volume, the group, pricing logic, and the Revolut/Uber screening protocol. Excellent behavioural follow-ups on "how do you check a number?" and "what is your block rule?". The Uber-order signal emerged from a patient excavation of "what are the rules for outcalls." Genuine, unprompted Gold signals on mental toll and blocking volume.

**Suggestions for next time:**
- The project/co-founder reveal happened before all the native-behavior excavation was complete. Next time, hold the reveal until the final 5 minutes — everything after the disclosure is post-pitch data and must be labelled as such. There's a clear shift in the transcript once the product is described: her answers become evaluative rather than descriptive.
- Several leading questions slipped through ("do you use calendar or something?", "do you write on the contact cards why you block them?"). Convert these into past-tense probes: "walk me through the last time you wanted to remember something about a client."
- Language barrier is significant. For future sessions with non-native speakers, consider offering the interviewee the option to speak in her native language with a translator or async text follow-up. A lot of texture was lost through halting English.
- Probe safety check-in protocol directly next time — she has a deposit gate but no described check-in. The Morocco story was an opening to ask "do you ever tell anyone where you're going?" and it wasn't taken.
- Keep the interviewer banter minimal. Some exchanges drifted into personal territory that generated social-desirability pressure.

### For the co-founder

- **Test the one-tap "block reason" primitive** with 2–3 beta users. If 700 blocks per user is representative, capturing 10% of them with reason-tags is a massive seed dataset for the collective blacklist.
- **Validate the Uber-order commitment pattern** — is this novel to JUSTYNA or used by others in the Geneva community? If common, it's a low-cost verification rail Scarlot can formalise.
- **Confirm the BMG-group access dependency** — what % of BMG subscribers are in the community chat? If most, then Scarlot's collective blacklist is in direct substitution for that layer, not for BMG listings.
- **Identify the non-native-speaker segment size** — what share of Lausanne/Zurich independent TDS are primarily non-FR/EN/DE? This has direct implications for the POC language stack.

### For next interview

- Probe **safety check-in behavior** explicitly — does anyone know where you are during an outcall? (Silence in this interview; must be resolved.)
